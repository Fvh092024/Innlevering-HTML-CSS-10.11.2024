Jupiter

Jupiter, a place of colossal storms, deadly radiation, and captivating beauty. A powerhouse whose mass is so great, it influences even the sun itself. Jupiter is fast becoming one of the most studied objects in our solar system - with 7 flybys, two orbiters (with one still in operation today) and two additional planned missions. The cosmic vacuum cleaner, Jupiter holds a vital role in protecting the earth from the incoming barrage of interstellar meteorites. The fifth planet from the sun, Jupiter is found on the outskirts of the asteroid belt, and sits between the orbits of Mars and Saturn. It is 778 million kilometers away from the sun on average, or 5.2 astronomical units, which means it's 5.2 times further from the sun than the earth's orbit from the sun. It completes a full orbit every 12 earth years. It is a massive planet, the largest in our solar system. It is so massive, its mass is 1/1000 that of the sun. That might not seem like a lot, but once you realize the sun contains 99.86% of all the mass in the solar system, you'll realize that Jupiter equals almost the remainder. Its mass is 2.5x the rest of the planets combined. Jupiter has 95 recognized moons, 4 of which (The Galilean moons) we’ll touch on briefly.

Io

Io is one of the most curious objects in our solar system. The innermost of Jupiter's big moons, it has plenty of features that sets it apart from anything else that we have ever seen, including volcanoes, aurorae, and a sulfur atmosphere. Io orbits very close to Jupiter, only 350 000 kilometers above Jupiter's cloud tops. This means that from Io's surface, Jupiter would appear 39 times bigger in the sky than our moon. Its orbit is in sync with two of the other Galilean moons, it orbits twice for every orbit of Europa, and 4 times for every orbit of Ganymede. This is what we call an orbital resonnance. Io is a pretty big moon, although it is the second smallest out of the Galilean moons. It is comparible in size to our own moon, and shares a similar density, meaning it has a similar amount of gravity. Io is volcanically active, and it is still the first and only place this has been visibly seen beyond earth. Its core is likely to be made of iron or iron sulfides, surrounded by a silicate rich mantle and crust. The core is not thought to be convecting them, as no magnetosphere has been detected around the moon.

Europa

Europa is the second and the smallest of the four Galilean moons, although it's still the sixth biggest moon in the entire solar system, just behind earths moon, with a diameter 90% that of our moon's. It takes Europa 3.5 days to orbit Jupiter once, twice as long as Io. Jupiters gravitational forces have a significant influence of the moons appearance, and what goes on under the surface. Europas surface is made predominantly of water ice. It looks very remarkable and distinctive due to its long, continous fractures and cracks. Despite being roughly the age of the solar system, Europa barely has any craters. It has less than 50 major craters, whereas earths moon has more than 5000 craters with a diameter of more than 25 kilometers. This indicates that Europas surface is constantly changing and reforming. Its icy surface is the smoothest surface of any known solid celestial object in the entire solar system. It is believed to host a body of water even bigger than our own. NASA’s Europa Clipper probe was launched on October 14th of this year. Its mission is to survey these oceans, scouting for potential life.

Ganymede

Ganymede, the third of Jupiters Galilean moons, and the largest moon that we know of. Like Europa, Ganymedes surface is a thick crust of water ice, extending 150 kilometers deep, underneath which is believed to lie a vast ocean of liquid water. The abundance of water on Ganymede reduces its average density. The ocean of Ganymede is so big that it contains more water than all the oceans and seas on Earth, and it's estimated to be 100 kilometers in depth on average, 10 times deeper than the Mariana Trench. All of this water means that Ganymede is only 50% rock, the rest being water and small amounts of metals and other ices. Interestingly, Ganymede has an atmosphere that contains oxygen. Now you might be wondering, with all this water and an oxygen atmosphere, is it possible that life exists on Ganymede? While it's certainly possible, there are some features of Ganymede that make this unlikely. The oxygen atmosphere is very thin. It is estimated to be 100 billion-500 billion times less than Earth's atmospheric pressure at sea level.

Callisto

Callisto is the third largest moon in the entire solar system, it's just smaller than Mercury at 4820km across, compared to Mercury's 4879. However, while its diameter is only 59 kilometers less than Mercury's, it's only 1/3rd of the planet's mass, meaning it's less dense and its gravity is a lot weaker. It's the second largest Galilean moon, and orbits much farther out than the other three, taking 17 days to do so at an average distance of 1.9 million kilometers. Even at this distance, it is still tidally locked to Jupiter, meaning the same side of the moon always faces its parent planet. Callisto has a very tenuous atmosphere composed of carbon dioxide. Its surface is one of the most ancient in the solar system, with evidence placing it at over 4 billion years old. You'll immediately notice the specled nature of Callisto, its surface is completely covered with various sized impact craters, more so than any other object we've observed. In fact, it's close to saturation, any new crater will probably just overlap another one at this point. Without any geological activity, cratering is perhaps the only process that has vastly been impacting Callisto's appearance over its lifetime.

Saturn

Saturn is big. It's a gas giant with an average radius about 9x that of Earth, making it the second biggest planet in our solar system. I say average radius, because its equatorial and polar radii differ by almost 10%. It's 60 000 kilometers at the equator, compared to 54 000 kilometers from pole to pole. With an average density of about 0.7g/cm^3, it is only 1/8 the average density of Earth. However, because its volume is so large, Saturn's mass is over 95 earths. This low density makes Saturn the lightest planet per cubic unit by far, and it is the only planet in the solar system that's less dense than water, about 30% less. So. if you had a bath toy of Saturn that shares the same density as the planet, it would float! Its yellow hue is believed to be due to ammonia crystals in the upper atmosphere. The rings are one of the highlights of the planet. Saturn has a prominent ring system that consists of 10 continuous main rings. They extend from 67 000 kilometers to 140 000 kilometers above Saturn's center. They're made up mostly of water ice, with small amounts of dust and rocks. While Saturn has 146 known moons, the only one we’ll take a look at this time will be Titan, one of the most intriguing bodies in the entire solar system.

Titan

Titan is Saturn’s largest moon and the second-largest in the solar system. It is unique due to its thick, nitrogen-rich atmosphere, which is mostly composed of nitrogen with traces of methane and other organic compounds. This dense atmosphere, combined with its cold surface temperature of around -179°C, makes Titan resemble an early version of Earth, with possible analogs for early life. Titan’s surface features large seas and lakes of liquid methane and ethane, with vast sand dunes made of hydrocarbon particles. Beneath its thick atmosphere, the moon has a solid icy surface and possibly an internal ocean of liquid water, suggesting potential conditions for life. Exploration of Titan has been primarily through NASA’s Cassini-Huygens mission. In 2005, the Huygens probe descended to the surface, sending back invaluable data, while Cassini orbited Saturn, studying Titan’s atmosphere and surface. Titan is of great interest to scientists due to its complex chemistry, which may offer insights into prebiotic conditions, and it remains a primary target for future missions like NASA’s Dragonfly, which is set to launch in the 2030s to explore Titan’s surface directly.

Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and one of the two "ice giants" in the solar system, alongside Neptune. It is unique for its extreme axial tilt—about 98 degrees—causing its poles to lie almost in the plane of its orbit. This results in extreme seasonal variations, with each pole experiencing long periods of continuous sunlight or darkness. Uranus is composed primarily of hydrogen, helium, and water, ammonia, and methane ices, giving it its blue-green color. It has a faint ring system and 27 known moons. The planet’s atmosphere is mainly hydrogen and helium, with traces of methane, which contribute to its distinct hue. Uranus has a relatively low internal heat output compared to other giant planets, which makes its weather patterns less intense, but still dynamic, with high-speed winds and storms. The planet was first discovered in 1781 by William Herschel, and although it has been visited only once, by Voyager 2 in 1986, Uranus remains a target for future exploration, offering potential insights into planetary formation and the conditions of ice giants.

Neptune

Look up into a clear night sky with your naked eye, what planets would you see? Technically you'd be able to see all of them at one time or another. All of them, apart from Neptune. It is the smallest of the gas giants, and also the farthest away. And it is a perplexing place. You would think a planet so far from the sun wouldn't have a dynamic atmosphere that exhibits ginormous storms and super fast winds. And yet, it does. Neptune is the only planet found through mathematical prediction. When Uranus was discovered and astronomers were plotting its orbit, they noticed that Uranus wasn't following their models. From the perturbed orbit of Uranus, Urbain Le Verrier in 1846 concluded that there must be another undiscovered planet, and he predicted where it should be. And remarkably, Johann Galle was able to find it only a degree away from the predicted point. It orbits at 30 astronomical units from the sun on average. 30 AUs in other words, is 4.47 billion kilometers. 4.5 billion kilometers is a considerable distance. Because of this long orbit, it takes a huge 165 years to orbit the sun once, which means we've only seen 1 neptunian year since its discovery.
